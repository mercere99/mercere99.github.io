What the techniques mean
Naked singleT1
A cell has only one digit left that doesn't conflict with its row, column, and box — place it.
Hidden singleT2
Within one row, column, or box, there is only one cell where a digit can still go — place it there, even if that cell has other candidates.
Locked candidatesT3
Inside a box, all of a digit's possible cells lie in a single row or column (or the reverse). The digit must land there, so remove it from the rest of that row/column (or box).
Naked pairT4
Two cells in a region allow exactly the same two digits. The pair uses those digits up, so remove them from every other cell in the region.
Hidden pairT5
Two digits can only appear in the same two cells of a region. Those cells must hold that pair, so strip all their other candidates.
Naked tripleT6 & Hidden tripleT7
The pair ideas extended to three cells and three digits. In a naked triple the cells needn't each hold all three digits — their options together just can't exceed the three.
Naked quadT8 & Hidden quadT9
The same with four cells and four digits. Rare, but purely mechanical to verify once spotted.
X-WingT10
Across two rows, a digit fits only in the same two columns, forming a rectangle. One diagonal pair of corners must hold it, so remove the digit from the rest of those two columns (also works with rows and columns swapped).
Skyscraper / kiteT11
Two regions each have exactly two spots for a digit ("strong links"). If one end of each link sees the other, then one of the two remaining ends must hold the digit — remove it from any cell that sees both of those ends.
XY-WingT12
A pivot cell with candidates XY sees one cell with XZ and one with YZ. Whichever digit the pivot takes, one pincer becomes Z — remove Z from cells that see both pincers.
XYZ-WingT13
Like XY-Wing, but the pivot holds XYZ itself. Z is guaranteed among the three cells, so it's removed only from cells that see the pivot and both pincers.
SwordfishT14 & JellyfishT15
X-Wing extended to three (Swordfish) or four (Jellyfish) rows and columns. Each base line may hold the digit in two or more spots, as long as together they stay inside the matching columns (or rows).
